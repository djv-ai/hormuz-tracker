TRACKER GUIDE

STRAIT OF HORMUZ · MARITIME INTELLIGENCE SYSTEM · v3

WHAT IS THIS TRACKER?

This tool monitors maritime vessel traffic through the Strait of Hormuz — one of the world's most strategically critical waterways. Approximately 20% of the world's oil supply passes through this strait daily, making it a key indicator of global energy security and geopolitical stability.

The tracker displays vessels moving through three zones: the Persian Gulf (upstream), the Strait of Hormuz narrows, and the Gulf of Oman (downstream). It monitors traffic flow, detects anomalies, flags vessels that turn off their transponders, and alerts you when traffic is returning to normal or being disrupted.

In simulation mode, vessels follow mathematically verified navigable routes based on real nautical charts. With an AIS key (free from aisstream.io), real live vessel positions replace the simulation.

THE THREE ZONES

PERSIAN GULF

The upstream zone to the west. Vessels here are either loading at terminals (Kuwait, Saudi Arabia, Qatar, UAE, Iran) or departing loaded toward the strait.

High vessel counts here indicate normal loading operations. A sudden drop can signal port closures or sanctions pressure.

STRAIT NARROWS

The critical chokepoint. Only ~3km wide for each lane at the narrowest point near the Musandam Peninsula (Oman). All vessels must transit through this corridor.

The Traffic Separation Scheme (TSS) divides the strait into an inbound lane (vessels entering the Gulf) and an outbound lane (vessels leaving). The amber dashed line marks the separation zone.

GULF OF OMAN

The downstream zone to the east. Vessels here are fully loaded and outbound heading toward the Indian Ocean, or inbound and empty arriving to load.

Traffic density here reflects successful transit through the strait. Monitoring this zone shows you what made it through.

VESSEL TYPES

VESSEL COLOURS & TYPES

Cyan — Tanker / LNG Carrier: Oil tankers and liquefied natural gas carriers. The most strategically significant vessels. A loaded VLCC (Very Large Crude Carrier) can carry 2 million barrels of crude oil.

Green — Cargo / Container: General cargo, bulk carriers, container ships. Carry manufactured goods, grain, and other commodities in and out of Gulf states.

Red — Naval / Military: Warships and naval vessels. Presence indicates active military operations or escort missions in the region.

Amber — Unknown: Vessels whose type cannot be determined from AIS data alone. Require further investigation.

Orange (flashing) — Dark Vessel: A vessel that has turned off its AIS transponder. This is a significant anomaly — see the Dark Vessels section below.

INTERACTING WITH VESSELS

Underway vessels appear as directional arrows rotated to their heading. Anchored vessels show as filled circles. Each leaves a faint coloured trail showing recent path — useful for spotting course changes.

Hover any vessel on the map for a tooltip showing name, type, flag, speed, and destination. Click it (or its entry in the vessel list) to pin it selected and view full details — MMSI, IMO, DWT, zone, and transit status — in the right panel.

⚠ DARK VESSELS — AIS TRANSPONDER OFF

WHAT IS A DARK VESSEL?

All commercial vessels over 300 gross tonnes are legally required under international maritime law (SOLAS) to broadcast their position via AIS (Automatic Identification System) at all times.

A "dark vessel" is one that has deliberately turned off or disabled its AIS transponder while at sea. This causes the vessel to disappear from tracking systems.

When a vessel goes dark in this tracker, it disappears from the map, the DARK AIS counter increments (top bar), an orange alert fires, and the vessel is flagged in the vessel list with a pulsing orange indicator.

WHY DO VESSELS GO DARK?

Sanction evasion: Iranian, Russian, or Venezuelan tankers frequently disable AIS to hide origins or destinations when carrying sanctioned cargo such as Iranian crude oil.

Ship-to-ship transfers: Vessels meeting at sea to transfer cargo without port inspection — commonly used to obscure oil origins.

Military / intelligence operations: Naval vessels conducting sensitive operations.

Piracy avoidance: Occasionally legitimate — vessels in high-risk areas sometimes go dark to avoid detection by pirates, though this is rare in Hormuz.

In the Hormuz context, dark tankers are the primary red flag for sanction violations or covert oil transfers.

◈ DARK TRANSIT TRACKING

THE THREE-PHASE SEQUENCE

The tracker models a full suspected dark-transit passage through the strait — not just an isolated AIS loss event. A vessel may go through three phases:

Phase 1 — Visible: The vessel transits normally, broadcasting AIS with a known flag, speed, and heading.

Phase 2 — Blind: AIS is disabled near the narrows. The vessel disappears from the map. Its position and actions during this window are unknown.

Phase 3 — Reacquired: The vessel reappears on the opposite side of the strait — confirming it completed a transit while completely untracked.

A vessel that enters the blind phase inbound and reappears outbound (or vice versa) has traversed the most sensitive chokepoint with no positional record. This is the primary signature of deliberate AIS manipulation for transit concealment — commonly associated with sanctions evasion or covert cargo transfer.

READING DARK TRANSIT ALERTS

When a completed dark transit is confirmed, the alert feed shows a DARK TRANSIT COMPLETE event — distinct from a simple VESSEL REAPPEARED, which flags a transponder gap where the vessel returned to the same side (possible equipment fault, not a confirmed crossing).

DARK TRANSIT COMPLETE
MT PERSIAN PRIDE reacquired SE of narrows. Direction: INBOUND → OUTBOUND. 14 min blind window. Flag: IRN.

The vessel is temporarily highlighted in amber in the vessel list with a TRANSIT badge and floated to the top. Clicking it shows the transit direction — e.g. INBOUND → OUTBOUND — in the detail panel under TRANSIT.

The highlight fades after approximately 30 seconds. The transit direction remains visible in the detail panel as long as the vessel is selected.

LEFT PANEL — TRAFFIC FLOW

FLOW BARS

Four horizontal bars show the current split of vessel activity:

Inbound → Gulf: Vessels transiting eastward into the Persian Gulf (typically empty tankers arriving to load, or cargo ships delivering goods to Gulf states).

Outbound → Sea: Vessels transiting westward out through the strait into the Gulf of Oman (typically full tankers departing with crude oil or LNG).

Slow / Anchored: Vessels moving under 3 knots or stationary. An unusually high anchored count can indicate vessels waiting due to port congestion, political tension, or sanctions.

Dark / No AIS: Vessels that have gone silent. The bar fills proportionally to how many are dark simultaneously.

SPARKLINE CHART

The wave chart below the flow bars shows vessel count over the last 60 update ticks — a real-time view of whether traffic is rising, falling, or stable.

The amber dashed line is the baseline average (~22–26 vessels). Blue line falling below it signals disruption; recovering back to it signals normalisation. This is your primary "is the strait open?" indicator.

RIGHT PANEL — ANALYSIS & ALERTS

ANALYTICS PANELS

The Chokepoint Risk Index (0–100) is a composite score weighted across four factors: dark vessel rate (40%), slow vessel rate (20%), oil flow drop vs baseline (30%), and anchored proportion (10%). LOW / MODERATE / ELEVATED / CRITICAL thresholds reflect escalating concern.

The Oil & LNG Flow section estimates outbound crude in MBD and LNG in BCF/day based on active tanker count × average cargo × speed factor. The flow bar shows throughput relative to the 17 MBD normal baseline — green above 75%, amber 45–75%, red below.

The Regional Breakdown bars show destination distribution across East Asia, Europe, Gulf Ports, and Americas — useful for identifying which trade routes are active or suppressed.

LIVE AIS DATA — PROVIDERS & FALLBACK

SIMULATION VS LIVE MODE

By default the tracker runs in simulation mode (SIM MODE) — 168 vessels follow verified navigable routes with full alerts and analytics. This is fully functional without any external connection.

Switching to live mode overlays real vessel positions, names, speeds, and destinations from AIS networks on top of the simulation. Click the SIM MODE badge in the top bar to open the AIS panel, select a provider, enter your credentials, and click CONNECT.

If the live feed drops, the tracker automatically retries up to 3 times (15s, 30s, 60s delays) before falling back to ENH SIM (enhanced simulation). You can reconnect at any time by clicking the status badge and pressing CONNECT again.

AIS PROVIDERS

AISSTREAM.IO (recommended) — free WebSocket stream with satellite + coastal AIS coverage. Best coverage for the Persian Gulf and Hormuz region. Sign up free at aisstream.io using GitHub, generate an API key on your dashboard, and paste it into the KEY field.

AISHUB.NET (secondary) — community-sourced AIS aggregator. Select AISHUB.NET from the dropdown and enter your AISHub username in the field. Coverage in the Persian Gulf is spottier than aisstream.io as it relies on land-based volunteer receivers, but useful as a backup.

The status badge shows: LIVE AIS (connected) · RETRY x/3 (auto-reconnecting) · ENH SIM (fallback) · CONN ERROR (key invalid).

WHAT TO WATCH FOR — KEY SIGNALS

DISRUPTION SIGNALS

Tanker count drops below 8 (from normal ~13)

Average speed falls below 8 knots

Anchored count rises above 6 vessels

Multiple dark vessels simultaneously

Outbound flow stops while inbound continues (loading but not departing)

RECOVERY SIGNALS

Vessel count rising back toward baseline

Average speed returning to 11-14 knots

Outbound tankers resuming normal flow

Dark vessels reappearing with AIS restored

Sparkline trending upward back to the amber baseline

GEOPOLITICAL CONTEXT

Traffic changes at Hormuz often reflect global events: US-Iran tensions, OPEC+ production decisions, Red Sea disruptions forcing rerouting, sanctions enforcement, and military exercises all leave a signature in the traffic data.

Use this tracker alongside news sources for the full picture.
