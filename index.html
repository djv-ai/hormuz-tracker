TRACKER GUIDE

STRAIT OF HORMUZ · MARITIME INTELLIGENCE SYSTEM · v3

WHAT IS THIS TRACKER?

This tool monitors maritime vessel traffic through the Strait of Hormuz — one of the world's most strategically critical waterways. Approximately 20% of the world's oil supply passes through this strait daily, making it a key indicator of global energy security and geopolitical stability.

The tracker displays vessels moving through three zones: the Persian Gulf (upstream), the Strait of Hormuz narrows, and the Gulf of Oman (downstream). It monitors traffic flow, detects anomalies, flags vessels that turn off their transponders, and alerts you when traffic is returning to normal or being disrupted.

In simulation mode, vessels follow mathematically verified navigable routes based on real nautical charts. With an AIS key (free from aisstream.io), real live vessel positions replace the simulation.

THE THREE ZONES

PERSIAN GULF

The upstream zone to the west. Vessels here are either loading at terminals (Kuwait, Saudi Arabia, Qatar, UAE, Iran) or departing loaded toward the strait.

High vessel counts here indicate normal loading operations. A sudden drop can signal port closures or sanctions pressure.

STRAIT NARROWS

The critical chokepoint. Only ~3km wide for each lane at the narrowest point near the Musandam Peninsula (Oman). All vessels must transit through this corridor.

The Traffic Separation Scheme (TSS) divides the strait into an inbound lane (vessels entering the Gulf) and an outbound lane (vessels leaving). The amber dashed line marks the separation zone.

GULF OF OMAN

The downstream zone to the east. Vessels here are fully loaded and outbound heading toward the Indian Ocean, or inbound and empty arriving to load.

Traffic density here reflects successful transit through the strait. Monitoring this zone shows you what made it through.

VESSEL TYPES

VESSEL COLOURS & TYPES

Cyan — Tanker / LNG Carrier: Oil tankers and liquefied natural gas carriers. The most strategically significant vessels. A loaded VLCC (Very Large Crude Carrier) can carry 2 million barrels of crude oil.

Green — Cargo / Container: General cargo, bulk carriers, container ships. Carry manufactured goods, grain, and other commodities in and out of Gulf states.

Red — Naval / Military: Warships and naval vessels. Presence indicates active military operations or escort missions in the region.

Amber — Unknown: Vessels whose type cannot be determined from AIS data alone. Require further investigation.

Orange (flashing) — Dark Vessel: A vessel that has turned off its AIS transponder. This is a significant anomaly — see the Dark Vessels section below.

VESSEL MARKERS EXPLAINED

Each vessel is shown as a circle with a directional arrow pointing in the direction of travel. The arrow rotates to show the vessel's current heading.

Hover over any vessel on the map to see its name, type, flag state, speed, and destination.

Click any vessel on the map or in the vessel list (left panel) to select it and see full details in the right panel — including MMSI number, IMO number, DWT (deadweight tonnage), and current zone.

Each vessel also leaves a coloured trail showing its recent path. Trails help you see direction of movement and identify vessels that have changed course unexpectedly.

⚠ DARK VESSELS — AIS TRANSPONDER OFF

WHAT IS A DARK VESSEL?

All commercial vessels over 300 gross tonnes are legally required under international maritime law (SOLAS) to broadcast their position via AIS (Automatic Identification System) at all times.

A "dark vessel" is one that has deliberately turned off or disabled its AIS transponder while at sea. This causes the vessel to disappear from tracking systems.

When a vessel goes dark in this tracker, it disappears from the map, the DARK AIS counter increments (top bar), an orange alert fires, and the vessel is flagged in the vessel list with a pulsing orange indicator.

WHY DO VESSELS GO DARK?

Sanction evasion: Iranian, Russian, or Venezuelan tankers frequently disable AIS to hide origins or destinations when carrying sanctioned cargo such as Iranian crude oil.

Ship-to-ship transfers: Vessels meeting at sea to transfer cargo without port inspection — commonly used to obscure oil origins.

Military / intelligence operations: Naval vessels conducting sensitive operations.

Piracy avoidance: Occasionally legitimate — vessels in high-risk areas sometimes go dark to avoid detection by pirates, though this is rare in Hormuz.

In the Hormuz context, dark tankers are the primary red flag for sanction violations or covert oil transfers.

LEFT PANEL — TRAFFIC FLOW

FLOW BARS

Four horizontal bars show the current split of vessel activity:

Inbound → Gulf: Vessels transiting eastward into the Persian Gulf (typically empty tankers arriving to load, or cargo ships delivering goods to Gulf states).

Outbound → Sea: Vessels transiting westward out through the strait into the Gulf of Oman (typically full tankers departing with crude oil or LNG).

Slow / Anchored: Vessels moving under 3 knots or stationary. An unusually high anchored count can indicate vessels waiting due to port congestion, political tension, or sanctions.

Dark / No AIS: Vessels that have gone silent. The bar fills proportionally to how many are dark simultaneously.

SPARKLINE CHART

The wave chart below the flow bars shows vessel count over the last 60 update ticks — giving you a real-time trend of whether traffic is increasing, decreasing, or stable.

The amber dashed line is the baseline average (~22-26 vessels). When the blue line dips significantly below the amber line, it signals disruption. When it recovers back to or above the amber line, traffic is returning to normal.

Think of this as your primary "is the strait open?" indicator at a glance.

RIGHT PANEL — ANALYSIS & ALERTS

NORMALITY ANALYSIS

The trend arrow and label compares current traffic to the 7-day baseline:

↑ Above baseline: More vessels than normal — traffic recovering or unusually high activity. Green.

→ Near baseline: Within ±12% of normal. Amber. This is the healthy state.

↓ Below baseline: Fewer vessels than normal — disruption, sanctions, or geopolitical event reducing traffic. Red.

The hourly bar chart shows vessel counts for each of the last 16 hours, with the amber baseline reference. Recent hours are shown in bright cyan.
